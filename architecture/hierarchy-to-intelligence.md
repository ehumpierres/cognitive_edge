# From Hierarchy to Intelligence: The Block Model

> Source: Sequoia Capital / Block (April 2026)
> https://sequoiacap.com/article/from-hierarchy-to-intelligence/

## The 2000-Year Problem

Every large organization faces the same constraint the Roman Army solved: how do you coordinate thousands of people with limited communication?

**The answer has always been hierarchy:**
- Roman Army: 8 → 80 → 480 → 5,000 (contubernium → century → cohort → legion)
- Prussian General Staff (1806): Created middle management — "professionals whose purpose was to route information, pre-compute decisions, and maintain alignment"
- Railroads (1850s): First org chart (McCallum, NY & Erie Railroad)
- Scientific Management (Taylor): Functional pyramid optimized for efficiency
- Matrix Organizations (1959): McKinsey's framework for global scale
- Modern tech experiments: Spotify squads, Holacracy, Valve flat structure

**The constraint**: A leader can effectively manage 3-8 people. More layers = slower information flow. Every organizational innovation for 2000 years has been working around this tradeoff without breaking it.

## What's Different Now

> "We're questioning the underlying assumption: that organizations have to be hierarchically organized with humans as the coordination mechanism. Instead, we intend to replace what the hierarchy does."

Most companies using AI: give everyone a copilot → existing structure works slightly better

Block's approach: **build the company as an intelligence (mini-AGI)**

## The Two World Models

### 1. Company World Model
Block is remote-first. Everything creates artifacts: decisions, discussions, code, designs, plans, problems, progress — all machine-readable.

In traditional companies, a manager's job is to know what's happening and relay context up/down the chain. In a remote-first company where work is already recorded, AI can build and maintain that picture continuously:
- What's being built
- What's blocked
- Where resources are allocated
- What's working, what isn't

> "That's the information the hierarchy used to carry. The company world model carries it instead."

### 2. Customer World Model
Money is the most honest signal in the world.

- People lie on surveys
- They ignore ads
- They abandon carts
- But when they spend, save, send, borrow, or repay — that's the truth

Block sees both sides of millions of transactions daily (Cash App = buyer, Square = seller) plus merchant operational data. This creates per-customer, per-merchant understanding built from honest signal that compounds.

> "The richer the signal, the better the model. The better the model, the more transactions. The more transactions, the richer the signal."

## The Four Things to Build

### 1. Capabilities
Atomic financial primitives: payments, lending, card issuance, banking, BNPL, payroll.

- **Not products** — building blocks
- Hard to acquire and maintain (network effects, regulatory permission)
- No UIs of their own
- Reliability, compliance, and performance targets

### 2. World Model
Two sides:
- **Company world model**: How the company understands itself — replaces information flow through management layers
- **Customer world model**: Per-customer, per-merchant, per-market representation from proprietary transaction data

Starts with raw transaction data → evolves toward full causal and predictive models.

### 3. Intelligence Layer
**Composes capabilities into solutions for specific customers at specific moments, delivered proactively.**

Example 1: Restaurant's cash flow tightening ahead of seasonal dip the model has seen before → intelligence layer composes short-term loan + adjusted repayment schedule → surfaces it before merchant thinks to look for financing.

Example 2: Cash App user's spending shifts in way associated with moving to new city → intelligence layer composes new direct deposit setup + Cash App Card with boosted categories for new neighborhood + savings goal calibrated to updated income.

> "No product manager decided to build either solution. The capabilities existed. The intelligence layer recognized the moment and composed them."

### 4. Interfaces
Hardware and software delivery surfaces: Square, Cash App, Afterpay, TIDAL, bitkey, proto.

Important, but not where value is created. **The value is in the model and the intelligence.**

## The Inverted Org Structure

**Traditional company**: Intelligence spread throughout people, hierarchy routes it.

**This model**: Intelligence lives in the system. People are on the edge.

### The Edge
Where the intelligence makes contact with reality:
- Reach into places the model can't go yet
- Sense things the model can't perceive: intuition, cultural context, trust dynamics, "the feeling in a room"
- Make calls the model shouldn't make: ethical decisions, novel situations, high-stakes moments

> "The edge doesn't need layers of management to coordinate it. The world model gives every person at the edge the context they need to act without waiting for information to travel up and down a chain of command."

## Three Roles Only

### 1. Individual Contributors (ICs)
- Build and operate capabilities, model, intelligence layer, interfaces
- Deep specialists in a specific layer
- World model provides context that manager used to provide
- Make decisions about their layer without waiting to be told

### 2. Directly Responsible Individuals (DRIs)
- Own specific cross-cutting problems/opportunities and customer outcomes
- Example: Own merchant churn in specific segment for 90 days
- Full authority to pull resources from multiple teams as needed
- May persist on certain problems or move to solve new ones

### 3. Player-Coaches
- Combine building with developing people
- Replace traditional manager whose primary job was information routing
- Still write code, build models, design interfaces
- Invest in growth of people around them
- **Don't spend days in status meetings, alignment sessions, priority negotiations**
- World model handles alignment, DRI structure handles strategy/priority
- Player-coach handles craft and people

## No Permanent Middle Management

> "Everything else the old hierarchy did, the system coordinates, and everyone is empowered, with a role that's much closer to the work and the customer."

## The Roadmap Inversion

Traditional: Product managers hypothesize about what to build next (ultimate limiting factor)

This model: When intelligence layer tries to compose a solution and can't because capability doesn't exist, that failure signal IS the roadmap.

> "Customer reality generates the backlog directly."

## The Strategic Question

> "What does your company understand that is genuinely hard to understand, and is that understanding getting deeper every day?"

**If the answer is nothing**: AI is just a cost optimization story. Cut headcount, improve margins, eventually get absorbed by something smarter.

**If the answer is deep**: AI doesn't augment your company. It reveals what your company actually is.

Block's answer: The economic graph — millions of merchants and consumers, both sides of every transaction, financial behavior in real time. That understanding compounds every second.

## The Core Insight

> "Companies move fast or slow based on information flow. Hierarchy and middle management impede information flow. For two thousand years, we have had no real alternative. Eight soldiers sharing a tent needed a decanus. Eighty men needed a centurion. Five thousand needed a legate. The question was never whether you needed layers. The question was whether humans were the only option for what those layers do. They aren't anymore."
